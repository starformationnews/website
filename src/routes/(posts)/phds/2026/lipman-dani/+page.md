The innermost region of the Milky Way, the Central Molecular Zone (CMZ), is the central reservoir of the Galaxy, where dense gas accumulates and orbits at a radius of $R_\text{gal}\sim$ 150 pc. Modeling the 3D orbital dynamics of our Galaxy’s CMZ holds key information to understanding galactic energy cycles and evolution, including the onset of AGN activity and nuclear starbursts. Previous observational and methodological constraints have been insufficient to distinguish between existing theoretical geometries. In this dissertation, I present new dust extinction techniques to determine near/far positions of all molecular clouds in the CMZ. I then build a Bayesian framework to combine my results with multiwavelength datasets and find posterior near/far likelihoods for cloud locations. Based on these posterior functions, I create a best-fitting 3D orbital model for the CMZ, and present the first top-down view of the CMZ with projected line of sight distance estimates for all clouds. My results show a structure of nested elliptical orbits, and reveal striking line of sight asymmetries with gas favoring the near side of the CMZ. I also explore new, high-resolution molecular data from ALMA, using both visual and algorithmic methods to identify and catalog hundreds of high-velocity compact clouds, some of which may connect nuclear-inflowing material to large-scale orbits. Lastly, I describe the design and production of new magneto-hydrodynamic simulations, incorporating a novel subgrid refinement scheme to achieve 1 M$_\odot$ resolutions, matching sub-parsec spatial scales of current observational datasets. My results indicate the need for more complex modeling of the CMZ's structure with updated observational and theoretical priors, including future simulation work to constrain mass and energy budgets in the CMZ.
