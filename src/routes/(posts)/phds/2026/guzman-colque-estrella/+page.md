Over the past decade, our understanding of massive star formation has significantly evolved with the discovery of explosive outflows—energetic, short-lived phenomena that coexist with accretion-driven bipolar outflows. Initially identified in Orion BN/KL, these events exhibit distinctive morphological and kinematic properties that suggest an extreme dynamical origin, differing from the classical scenario of collimated ejection. In this thesis, the nature of molecular outflows associated with star-forming regions was investigated, with particular emphasis on explosive outflows and their implications for models of massive star formation. This study is based on the analysis of high-angular-resolution submillimeter interferometric observations obtained with ALMA and SMA toward four regions: IRAS\,16076--5134, DR\,21, Sh2--106, and IRAS\,15398--3359.

The thesis is organized into four main parts. Part I (Chapters 1--3) provides a theoretical and observational review of star formation, covering the fundamentals of molecular clouds and gravitational collapse, as well as scenarios for massive star formation and the properties of molecular outflows, with particular emphasis on explosive outflows as key phenomena in the dynamics of massive star-forming regions. Part II (Chapters 4--7) details the procedures used to analyze the millimeter continuum and CO line emission observations presented in this work. These chapters describe how continuum sources are identified and characterized, including the derivation of physical parameters such as dust mass. Methods to identify filamentary structures in CO emission and to estimate physical properties such as mass, kinetic energy, and momentum are also presented. In addition, the procedure to determine the center of an explosive outflow and to estimate the occurrence rate of such events in the Milky Way is described. Part III (Chapters 8--12) presents the main results, including the discovery of a new explosive outflow in IRAS\,16076--5134, the confirmation of the explosive outflow in DR\,21, the identification of two continuum sources in Sh2--106, and the detection of a precessing outflow in IRAS\,15398--3359, concluding with a synthesis of the findings and their implications for massive star formation. Finally, an Appendix describes the calibration and imaging procedures used to obtain continuum maps and spectral cubes from ALMA and SMA observations.

The results of this thesis support the hypothesis that explosive molecular outflows are direct observational manifestations of extreme dynamical processes in massive star-forming regions, such as close gravitational encounters, dynamical interactions, or protostellar mergers. These processes, absent from classical models, introduce an alternative pathway for the formation of high-mass stars. This work suggests that such events may be considerably more frequent than previously assumed, potentially involving a significant fraction (at least one out of every two) of high-mass protostars.
