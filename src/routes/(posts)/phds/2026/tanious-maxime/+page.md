Providing unprecedented sensitivities, spatial, and spectral resolutions, the latest generation of telescopes (NOEMA, ALMA, JWST) can now study the evolution of interstellar matter from star-forming regions to protoplanetary disks. Rich in gas and dust, protoplanetary disks are formed around young stars and constitute the cradles of planetary formation. In this PhD thesis, I aimed at better characterizing the evolution of young protoplanetary disks (a.k.a. Class I sources) in their natal environment. Class I sources are made of gas- and dust-rich disk(s) potentially forming planets, but are still embedded in cloud envelope material. Over the past decade, several spectral surveys probed the chemistry of Class 0 and Class II objects. However, little is known about the intermediate Class I stage chemistry. Secondly, using a variety of radiative transfer methods, I showed that the streamer is massive and should replenish multiple times the disk's material, suggesting big structural impacts on the star-disk system. Finally, by deriving chemical abundance ratios, I provided evidence that the streamer likely retains a chemical footprint from the prestellar core from which it originated, suggesting an interstellar heritage from the core to the disk through the streamer. This work underlines how crucial it is to study the natal environment of protoplanetary disks to better understand their evolution, both from a physical and chemical point of view.
