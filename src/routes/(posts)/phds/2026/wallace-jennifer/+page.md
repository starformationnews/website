Stars form within the dense interstellar medium (ISM), as the gas from large molecular clouds fragments and condenses into pre- and protostellar cores. Although it is known that stellar clusters originate from these groups of dense cores, the star formation process as shaped by gas fragmentation, core mass accretion, as well as interaction with the surrounding ISM is still a topic of ongoing investigation. Observations with sufficient spatial resolution for resolving core populations have largely been restricted to relatively small protostellar clusters at heliocentric distances $\leq$ 500 pc. However, the larger-scale surveys that scan the Galactic plane fail to resolve individual star-forming cores. In this dissertation, I bridge this gap by presenting high resolution ALMA (Atacama Large Millimeter/sub-millimeter Array) dust continuum observations of more than 1000 star-forming regions covering a large range of Galactic environments that I use to catalog and characterize star-forming structures throughout the Milky Way. First, I report on the discovery of filamentary structures in the Sgr E region, a star forming complex located at the nexus of our Central Molecular Zone (CMZ) and the Galactic bar 'far' dust lane. Then I present the hierarchical decomposition of the 1.3 mm continuum structure for 900 dense clumps observed throughout the Galactic disk which I use to statistically characterize the internal mass evolution of star-forming clumps down to the core scale. Finally, I introduce the ALMA CMZ Exploration Survey (ACES) source catalog, a census of compact 3 mm continuum structures extracted from a high-resolution ($\sim0.1$ pc) ALMA mosaic with coverage of the CMZ greatly exceeding previous surveys at a similar wavelength and resolution.
