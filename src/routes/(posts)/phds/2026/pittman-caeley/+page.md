Classical T Tauri stars (CTTS) and their protoplanetary disks provide a window into the critical epoch of exoplanet formation. CTTS interact with the inner disk through the magnetospheric connection, with their strong magnetic fields truncating the disk and channeling material onto the star in the process of magnetospheric accretion. This dissertation utilizes signatures of accretion and stellar rotation in ultraviolet through infrared observations to model the inner regions of the largest sample of CTTS to date.

First, I model accretion in the HST ULLYSES sample of 67 CTTS. I find that typical magnetospheres are almost half the size as has been traditionally assumed, which reveals a simpler explanation for ultra-short-period exoplanet formation. I present evidence of rotational modulation of accretion emission, indicating non-axisymmetric hotspots that persist for multiple stellar rotation periods. I show that accretion emission is diverse, producing varied high-energy radiation environments that should be considered when interpreting the chemical signatures observed in different systems with JWST and ALMA.

Next, I measure stellar rotation periods and corotation radii ($R_{\rm corot}$) for a subset of 47 CTTS from my sample. I perform the first large-scale observational tests of the top theorized mechanisms of angular momentum transfer in the inner disk. I find that most CTTS are ""slow rotators"" rather than being in equilibrium spin states, and this is consistent with efficient angular momentum loss processes that maintain slow spin rates throughout the CTTS phase. Additionally, while dipper stars have been assumed to have magnetospheric truncation radii ($R_{\rm mag}$) that are co-located with $R_{\rm corot}$, I find that dippers occur over the full range of $R_{\rm mag}/R_{\rm corot}$, requiring a paradigm shift for understanding the origins of dipper signatures. 

Finally, I expand my sample and incorporate 3D spatial information to examine the influence of the large-scale natal cloud on small-scale CTTS properties. I demonstrate that the viewing inclinations of CTTS magnetospheres are spatially correlated over scales of ~3 pc in Lupus. I propose that this spatial dependency originates from large-scale external influences on the region combined with filamentary collapse. This dissertation concludes with a discussion of implications for studies of accretion, angular momentum transport, and exoplanet formation and evolution.
