In this thesis, I explore the power of time-resolved data to investigate the dynamic lives of young stars, demonstrating that variability is a fundamental diagnostic of stellar physics. Intermediate-mass young stellar objects, including Herbig Ae/Be stars, occupy a critical regime between low-mass T Tauri stars and massive protostars. Through time-resolved spectroscopy and photometry, I show that rotational periods can be recovered across this mass range with no significant dependence on mass, and that accretion and outflow processes are highly dynamic. By tracking variability, it is possible to map otherwise inaccessible structures in the inner disk and circumstellar environment, probing regions beyond the reach of conventional imaging. Accounting for photometric variability is essential for accurate stellar parameters, with derived luminosities significantly revised for several stars when variability is properly included.

Extending this methodology to the cluster scale, I combine Gaia DR3 astrometry, North-PHASE multi-epoch photometry, and infrared diagnostics to produce the most complete membership census of NGC 2264 to date. While Gaia defines the cluster’s kinematic framework, variability completes the picture, identifying 145 additional members whose proper motions deviate from the cluster mean but show clear signs of youth, revealing the full spatial and evolutionary complexity of the cluster. Manual inspection of light curves shows that 60% of members display measurable variability, far exceeding automated detection rates. Both accreting classical T Tauri stars and diskless weak-lined T Tauri (WTTS) stars exhibit detectable periodicity, with WTTS showing lower amplitudes but equally recoverable rotation periods, opening the way to future studies of rotational evolution across different accretion regimes.

Taken together, these results demonstrate that variability is a universal and unbiased probe, linking rotation, accretion, and circumstellar structure from individual stars to entire clusters. This work establishes a framework for future studies of stellar evolution and inner-disk physics in pre–main-sequence stars, showing how time-domain observations can effectively map structures otherwise inaccessible to direct imaging.
