Theoretical astrophysics has lost one of its most brilliant voices with the passing of Prof. Vladimir Pavlovich Grinin, whose work laid essential foundations for the physical interpretation of variability in young stellar objects.

Grinin’s scientific legacy rests primarily on his theoretical treatment of circumstellar matter around pre–main-sequence stars. Working at the Pulkovo Astronomical Observatory and Saint Petersburg State University, he developed physically consistent models describing how dust, gas and radiation interact in the immediate environments of young stars. His work clarified how disk geometry, variable extinction and scattering processes can give rise to the complex photometric and polarimetric behaviour observed in Herbig Ae/Be and UX Orionis-type stars.

A defining feature of Grinin’s approach was his use of minimal but physically motivated assumptions. Rather than relying on phenomenological descriptions, he constructed models grounded in radiative transfer, disk structure and dust physics, enabling robust links between theory and observable signatures. His theoretical framework for UX Ori variability, attributing deep fading events to orbiting or outflowing dust structures, remains a cornerstone of the field.

Grinin collaborated extensively with international groups, including projects with the Max Planck Institute for Radio Astronomy in Bonn, the Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies in Dublin, and the National Institute for Astrophysics in Florence. Within these collaborations, he extended his previous work to disk winds and inner-disk regions, contributing to models that connect line emission, accretion and outflows at sub-astronomical-unit scales. These studies provided a theoretical context for high-resolution interferometric and spectroscopic observations, shaping how such data are interpreted today. Notably, he remained scientifically active until his final days, continuing to work on theoretical problems, collaborating with colleagues and engaging critically with new observational results.

Grinin was widely respected for his intellectual discipline and conceptual clarity. His theoretical insights continue to influence research on protoplanetary disks and early stellar evolution, ensuring that his impact on astrophysics will endure well beyond his lifetime.
